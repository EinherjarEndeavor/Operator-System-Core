# New Medicaid Work Requirements in 2027: Complete Guide to Exemptions, Substance Use Treatment, and Survival Strategies

## The Cliff Notes Version

Starting January 1, 2027, if you're a U.S. adult aged 19-64 on Medicaid expansion, you'll need to work or participate in qualifying activities for **80 hours per month** to keep your coverage—or qualify for an exemption. The good news: **If you're in substance use disorder (SUD) treatment, you're exempt.** But there are important limitations and documentation requirements you need to understand to stay covered. This article walks you through everything.

---

## What Actually Changed: The "One Big Beautiful Bill" Act (H.R. 1)

On July 4, 2025, President Trump signed H.R. 1 (officially "One Big Beautiful Bill Act") into law. This is the first federal mandate in Medicaid's 60-year history to require work as a condition of coverage.

**Key fact:** H.R. 1 was signed July 4, 2025[1]. The Medicaid work requirements take effect January 1, 2027, though states can implement earlier if they choose[2].

This is not an elimination of Medicaid—it's a tightening of eligibility that will fundamentally change how approximately 4-5 million Americans stay covered.

---

## Who Must Meet the 80-Hour Requirement?

The rule applies to adults ages **19-64** who have Medicaid coverage through their state's Medicaid expansion (coverage for people earning 138% of the federal poverty level or less)[3].

**Crucial distinction:** The requirement does NOT apply to:
- Children under 19
- Adults 65+
- People on traditional (non-expansion) Medicaid
- People on Medicare

If you're in Oregon's OHP (Oregon Health Plan), you likely fall under expansion coverage, meaning you're subject to these requirements unless you qualify for an exemption[4][5].

---

## The 80-Hour Monthly Requirement: What Actually Counts?

You must complete **80 hours per month** of one or a combination of these activities[6]:

### ✓ Paid Employment
- Any type of job, full-time or part-time
- Self-employment or business income
- Must total at least 80 hours per month

### ✓ Income Equivalent to 80 Hours
- Earning at least **$580 per month** (80 hours × $7.25 federal minimum wage)
- Applies to self-employment and business income
- Seasonal workers: average monthly income over past 6 months of $580+[7]

### ✓ Community Service / Volunteering
- At least 80 hours per month
- Unpaid volunteer work at nonprofits, food banks, community organizations[8]

### ✓ Work Program or Job Training
- Participation in approved training programs
- SNAP Employment & Training (SNAP E&T)
- Educational job training programs[9]

### ✓ Educational Enrollment
- Enrollment in school, college, or training program at **least half-time** (40 hours per month minimum)[10]

### ✓ Mixing & Matching
You can combine activities. For example[11]:
- 40 hours paid work + 40 hours volunteering = meets requirement
- 60 hours paid work + 30 hours community service = meets requirement
- Any combination that totals 80 hours works

## The Substance Use Disorder (SUD) Treatment Exemption: The Complete Rule

**Here's the statutory language from H.R. 1 and federal Medicaid guidance:**

"Individuals participating in a **qualifying substance use disorder (SUD) treatment program** are exempt from work requirements."[12][13]

This is a **mandatory exemption**—meaning every state, including Oregon, must recognize it[14].

### What Qualifies as a Qualifying SUD Treatment Program?

Based on federal guidance and existing SNAP exemption language (which Medicaid incorporates), qualifying programs include[15]:

✓ **Intensive outpatient programs (IOPs)**
- Programs like CODA (Community Outreach & Addiction Services) where you attend multiple times per week[16]
- Your program coordinates treatment, counseling, and recovery support

✓ **Inpatient/Residential Treatment**
- Hospital-based addiction treatment
- Residential recovery facilities

✓ **Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT)**
- Methadone clinics
- Suboxone/buprenorphine programs
- Any FDA-approved medication treatment for opioid or alcohol use disorder

✓ **Outpatient Counseling & Therapy for SUD**
- One-on-one counseling
- Group therapy focused on substance use

✓ **Recovery Support Services**
- If delivered as part of a formal treatment program (not informal peer support)

### Critical Limitation: The Definition Matters

Under federal SNAP rules (which Medicaid is adopting), treatment programs must meet specific criteria. Historically, SNAP has limited exemptions to "private, non-profit treatment facilities and community mental health centers,"[17] which account for less than half of all SUD treatment facilities in the U.S.

**However**, for Medicaid, federal guidance is still being finalized. HHS is required to issue final regulations by **June 1, 2026**[18], six months before implementation. This means the exact definition of "qualifying" treatment programs will be clearer by mid-2026.

**Bottom line for you right now:** If you're in treatment at a licensed facility (like CODA), you almost certainly qualify. But get written confirmation from your provider once the final federal rules are published.

## How the Exemption Works in Practice

### Documentation & Verification

States don't need to require you to provide special documentation **at the time of your exemption claim**[19]. However, they may verify your participation through:

- **Electronic data matching** with treatment programs (states submit requests to providers)
- **Attendance records** from your treatment provider
- **Treatment plan documentation** from your provider
- **Provider certification** that you're actively participating

**Practical recommendation:** Ask CODA or your treatment provider for an **attestation letter** stating you're enrolled and actively participating in their SUD treatment program. Keep copies for your records.

### When They Check Your Exemption

Oregon will verify your exemption status at[20][21]:

1. **When you apply or renew OHP** (every 6 months starting January 2027)
2. **Upon request** if the state questions your eligibility
3. **Proactively** if state eligibility workers flag your account

You have a right to at least **30 days' notice** if the state questions your exemption before they can disenroll you[22].

## What Happens When Treatment Ends?

**Critical issue:** The exemption only lasts while you're actively in treatment.

Once you complete your SUD treatment program, the exemption ends[23]. You'll then need to:

1. **Meet the 80-hour work requirement** through employment, volunteering, education, or other qualifying activities, OR
2. **Qualify for another exemption** (pregnancy, disability, caregiving for children under 14, etc.)

**Strategic planning:** If you're in treatment now and anticipate completing, consider:
- Enrolling in educational programs (Portland Community College, for example) to stay exempt through educational enrollment[24]
- Building employment connections during treatment to ease transition to work
- Documenting disability diagnoses if applicable (depression, PTSD, anxiety) which qualify as medical frailty exemptions[25]

## Other Mandatory Exemptions (You May Qualify for Multiple)

You're also exempt if you are[26][27]:

✓ **Pregnant** or received postpartum Medicaid (within 12 months of pregnancy)

✓ **Parent/guardian/caregiver of dependent children age 13 or under**

✓ **Caregiver for an individual with a disability** (any age)

✓ **Medically frail**, which includes having[28]:
- A disability or blindness
- A substance use disorder (even if not in active treatment—this overlaps with SUD exemption)
- A serious mental illness or "disabling mental disorder"
- A physical, intellectual, or developmental disability
- A serious or complex medical condition

✓ **Disabled veteran** with a service-connected disability rating

✓ **American Indian/Alaska Native** from federally recognized tribe

✓ **Recently incarcerated** (within last 3 months)

✓ **Foster youth or former foster youth** (under age 26)

✓ **Enrolled in school, college, or training program at least half-time** (40+ hours per month)

✓ **Already meeting work requirements for TANF or SNAP** (if you're complying with those programs)

### The "Medical Frailty" Exemption: The Backdoor Route

For people with substance use disorder histories, the "medically frail" exemption may offer **additional protection beyond the SUD treatment exemption**[29]. This exemption covers people with:

- Substance use disorder (regardless of treatment status)
- Serious mental illness
- Complex medical conditions

The advantage: This exemption doesn't expire when treatment ends. However, **you'll need documentation** from a healthcare provider to establish medical frailty[30].

## How Oregon Is Implementing This: The State-Level Picture

### Implementation Timeline for Oregon

Oregon is preparing for January 1, 2027 implementation, but the process is chaotic[31]:

- **Federal rules finalized by:** June 1, 2026[32]
- **Oregon policy design deadline:** December 2025–January 2026[33]
- **IT system changes needed:** Extensive; Oregon's eligibility systems need overhaul
- **Additional staff required:** Oregon estimates needing 475+ new eligibility workers statewide[34]
- **Funding gap:** Oregon requested $340+ million to implement but may not receive adequate federal reimbursement[35]

### Risks for Oregon OHP Members

Because Oregon is understaffed and underfunded, the state will likely face[36]:

- **Administrative disenrollment:** People losing coverage due to paperwork failures, not actual ineligibility
- **Longer processing times** for exemption requests
- **Verification delays** that could cause temporary coverage gaps
- **Potential phased rollout:** Oregon may petition for delay until October 1, 2027 (their 1115 waiver expires September 2027)[37]

**What this means for you:** Keep meticulous records, respond promptly to any state requests, and don't assume coverage will continue without verification.

## Verification & Documentation: Practical Survival Strategies

### What You Must Do

1. **Monthly/Regular Record-Keeping**
   - If employed: Keep pay stubs
   - If volunteering: Get written hours from volunteer coordinator
   - If in school: Maintain enrollment proof
   - If in treatment: Get attendance records from provider

2. **Respond to State Requests Immediately**
   - Oregon will send notices by mail and at least one other method (text/email)
   - You have 30 days to respond
   - Non-response = automatic disenrollment[38]

3. **Provide Exemption Documentation**
   - For SUD treatment: Letter from CODA stating you're in active treatment
   - For disability: Recent diagnosis or disability determination letter
   - For caregiver status: Birth certificate of child or disability documentation of care recipient

4. **Stay in Contact with Your Provider**
   - Ensure your provider has your current contact information
   - Ask provider to notify you if state requests verification
   - Keep copies of all treatment program documentation

5. **Monitor Your OHP Account**
   - Check Oregon's OHP portal regularly
   - Sign up for email/text notifications
   - Don't ignore notices from Washington County DHS

### What Happens If You Miss the 30-Day Deadline

- Your application is denied (if applying), OR
- Your coverage ends at the end of the month[39]
- You're barred from subsidized marketplace insurance for 12 months[40]
- You can reapply, but must show 1-3 months of compliance with work requirements

## Potential Challenges & How to Handle Them

### Challenge #1: Treatment Program Not Recognized as "Qualifying"

**Problem:** Your provider may not be on state's approved list (if one exists)

**Solution:**
- Request written confirmation from OHA (Oregon Health Authority) that your program qualifies
- Provide OHA with program documentation (accreditation, licensing, curriculum)
- Legal aid organizations (Community Alliance of Tenants, Oregon Law Center) can advocate for you

### Challenge #2: Paperwork Burden on Your Provider

**Problem:** CODA staff may be overwhelmed with state verification requests; they might not provide timely documentation

**Solution:**
- Get documentation proactively before January 2027
- Ask about backup contacts (supervisor, HR) if primary contact is unavailable
- Follow up if documentation request goes unanswered for >5 business days

### Challenge #3: Your Exemption Is Denied

**Problem:** State denies your SUD treatment exemption despite active participation

**Solution:**
- Request a **fair hearing** (you have this right under federal law)[41]
- Contact community legal aid:
  - **Community Alliance of Tenants:** (503) 287-9486
  - **Oregon Law Center:** (503) 295-5422 (Portland)
  - **Legal Aid Services of Oregon:** (503) 224-4086
- File complaint with OHP (Oregon Health Authority)

### Challenge #4: You Lose Coverage Due to Administrative Error

**Problem:** State loses documentation or denies coverage in error

**Solution:**
- Request immediate reinstatement (states can reinstate coverage retroactively)
- File appeal/fair hearing request
- Seek help from legal aid to argue administrative failure

## After Treatment: Transition Planning

### The Critical Timeline

**Month 1-6 of treatment:** Enjoy exemption; focus on recovery

**Month 3-4 before expected discharge:** Begin transition planning
- Discuss with treatment counselor what comes next
- Research education options (PCC, vocational training)
- Build employment connections while in treatment

**Upon discharge from treatment:**
- Immediately enroll in qualifying activity, OR
- Ensure you have documented disability (for medical frailty exemption), OR
- Face loss of coverage within 30 days

### Strategic Exemption Stacking

You can layer exemptions. For example[42]:
- **Primary:** SUD treatment (active participation)
- **Secondary:** Enrollment in Portland Community College (half-time educational enrollment)
- **Tertiary:** Disability determination if you have co-occurring mental health conditions

Once treatment ends, you're still covered if enrolled in education. Once education ends, you may still be covered if you qualify as medically frail due to documented mental health conditions (depression, PTSD, anxiety disorders are often comorbid with SUD).

## Federal Projections: What We Know About Outcomes

Research published before final implementation shows[43]:

- **5+ million Americans** are projected to lose Medicaid by 2034 due to work requirements
- **58% of Oregon Medicaid expansion adults already work**[44]—suggesting work requirements may not increase employment but will increase administrative burden
- **Historical precedent (Arkansas 2018):** Work requirements caused 18,000 people (1 in 4 eligible) to lose coverage in 10 months[45]
- **No increase in employment observed** in states that implemented work requirements; requirements mainly screen out people who cannot document work[46]

**The bottom line:** This is primarily an administrative mechanism to reduce Medicaid enrollment, not an employment policy.

## Why This Matters & What's Actually Happening

The political framing is that work requirements "promote responsibility." The practical reality[47]:

- **58% of Oregon Medicaid expansion adults already work**
- **40% have serious mental illness or substance use disorder** (may have trouble documenting work due to health conditions)
- **15% are experiencing homelessness or housing instability** (may lack stable address for verification)

What this actually creates[48]:
- A verification system that screens people out for paperwork failures, not ineligibility
- Increased administrative costs that states can't afford
- Cycling on and off coverage, which destabilizes treatment adherence and employment

For individuals in SUD recovery specifically:
- The SUD treatment exemption is your protection—**use it**
- But plan for the end of treatment
- Connect to education, employment, or disability documentation before discharge
- Keep meticulous records

## A Note on Recovery & Advocacy

If you're 3 months sober and in intensive treatment, you're doing the hardest work. The bureaucratic work requirements that follow are easier than the real work you're doing in recovery.

The system is designed for administrative failures, not your success. Be intentional about documentation and follow-up. Your access to healthcare depends on it.

For policy advocates, decision-makers, and those interested in the bigger picture: Contact your state legislators and U.S. representatives. Provide testimony about how work requirements affect people in treatment. The final rules aren't written yet—advocacy still matters.
