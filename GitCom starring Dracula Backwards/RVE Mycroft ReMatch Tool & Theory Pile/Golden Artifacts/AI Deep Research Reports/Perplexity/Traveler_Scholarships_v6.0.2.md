## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This focused intelligence compiles **verified scholarships, grants, and tuition assistance programs** exclusively for formerly incarcerated and justice-impacted students. Funding ranges from **$500–$10,000+ per year**. Programs include **federal Pell Grants, nonprofit scholarships, state initiatives, and university-specific funds**. Most programs require acceptance to an accredited college/university + proof of incarceration status.

**Critical Timeline:** If pursuing education post-release, apply simultaneously to **Pell Grants (federal), state grants (Oregon Opportunity Grant), and targeted nonprofit scholarships (3–5 applications)** to maximize funding coverage.

## TIER 1: FEDERAL FUNDING (HIGHEST IMPACT)

### **Federal Pell Grants — Restored 2023**

**Program Name:** Pell Grant for Incarcerated & Formerly Incarcerated Students  
**Funder:** U.S. Department of Education  
**Description:** Restored in 2023 after 30-year ban (1994–2023). Provides need-based federal grant (does NOT require repayment). Currently available to incarcerated students in approved Prison Education Programs (PEPs) and ALL formerly incarcerated students pursuing postsecondary education. Maximum award: **$6,895/year (2024–2025 academic year)**.

**Cost:** Free to apply  
**Maximum Award:** $6,895/year (varies by Expected Family Contribution)  
**Eligibility:**
- U.S. citizen or eligible non-citizen
- Enrolled in accredited college, university, trade school, or community college
- Financial need (determined by FAFSA)
- Currently or formerly incarcerated (for justice-impacted priority)
- Have valid Social Security number
- Be in good academic standing or meet SAP (Satisfactory Academic Progress)

**How to Apply:**
1. Complete Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA) at **fafsa.gov**
2. List incarceration status in application narrative (when prompted: "Are you in foster care, homeless, or have experienced incarceration?")
3. Select school(s) where award applies
4. Award notification sent to school + student

**Timeline:**
- FAFSA opens: October 1 annually
- Priority deadline: December 31 (for financial aid priority)
- Applications accepted: Through June 30

**Life-Impact Weight:** 10/10  
**Priority Index:** 9.9  
**[IMMEDIATE PRIORITY - Federal Funding]**

**Action Steps:**
- Step 1: Gather documents (SSN, ID, tax info if available)
- Step 2: Go to fafsa.gov; create account with email
- Step 3: Complete FAFSA (takes 10–15 minutes)
- Step 4: List college(s); submit
- Step 5: Await award notification (2–4 weeks)
- Step 6: Financial aid office at college will apply Pell to your account

**Verification:** Official U.S. Department of Education program | Reinstated July 1, 2023 | Nationwide implementation  
**Source:** fafsa.gov | ED.gov | National Reentry Resource Center

### **Workforce Pell (NEW — Launching July 2026)**

**Program Name:** Workforce Pell Grants  
**Funder:** U.S. Department of Education  
**Description:** Expanded Pell eligibility to **short-term workforce training programs (8–15 weeks, 150–599 hours)**. Designed for high-skill, high-wage, in-demand job training. Incarcerated students in approved Workforce Pell programs will be eligible. Particularly valuable for credential-focused, rapid-entry jobs (healthcare, skilled trades, IT certifications).

**Cost:** Free to apply  
**Maximum Award:** TBD (expected $3,000–$5,000 for short-term programs)  
**Eligibility:**
- Enrolled in federally approved Workforce Pell program
- Program leads to recognized credential (certificate, license)
- Program is 8–15 weeks / 150–599 clock hours
- Currently or formerly incarcerated
- Meet general Pell eligibility criteria

**Timeline:**
- Rules finalized: December 2025 (AHEAD rulemaking)
- Implementation: July 1, 2026
- Colleges begin approvals: 2026 (2-stage state + federal process)

**Life-Impact Weight:** 8/10  
**Priority Index:** 8.2  
**[Forward Planning - Emerging Opportunity]**

**Action Steps:**
- Step 1: Watch for July 2026 launch announcements
- Step 2: Contact local community college (PCC, COCC, etc.) about Workforce Pell programs
- Step 3: If incarcerated, request information from education staff about eligible programs post-release
- Step 4: Prioritize short-term, high-wage credentials (healthcare, trades)
- Step 5: Apply to Pell + Workforce Pell simultaneously when eligible

**Verification:** Official Department of Education policy | December 2025 rulemaking finalized | AHEAD process complete  
**Source:** ED.gov | Jobs for the Future (JFF) policy analysis

## TIER 2: STATE-LEVEL GRANTS (OREGON-SPECIFIC)

### **Oregon Opportunity Grant**

**Program Name:** Oregon Opportunity Grant  
**Funder:** Oregon Student Assistance Commission (OSAC)  
**Description:** Need-based state grant for Oregon residents pursuing higher education (AA, BA, vocational certificates). No repayment required. Prioritizes low-income students. Available to formerly incarcerated students with Oregon residency.

**Cost:** Free to apply  
**Maximum Award:** $3,300–$3,900/year (varies by school type)  
**Eligibility:**
- Oregon resident (1+ year residency required; incarceration time does NOT count toward residency)
- Enrolled full-time or part-time in accredited Oregon school
- Financial need (determined by FAFSA)
- U.S. citizen or eligible non-citizen
- Satisfactory academic progress

**How to Apply:**
1. Complete FAFSA (fafsa.gov) — list Oregon school
2. Oregon schools automatically consider for OOG
3. Award notification sent to school

**Timeline:**
- FAFSA priority: December 31
- Awards: January–March (rolling)

**Life-Impact Weight:** 8/10  
**Priority Index:** 8.6  
**[IMMEDIATE PRIORITY - Oregon State Funding]**

**Action Steps:**
- Step 1: Verify Oregon residency (1+ year)
- Step 2: Complete FAFSA
- Step 3: Select Oregon college/university
- Step 4: Monitor OSAC.state.or.us for award status
- Step 5: Confirm with financial aid office

**Contact:**
- Website: oregonstudentaid.gov

## STRATEGY: MAXIMIZING SCHOLARSHIP SUCCESS

### **Application Timeline (Post-Release Path)**

**MONTH 1 (Upon/Shortly After Release):**
1. Enroll in GED program if needed (free: PCC, SOCC, USAHello)
2. Research college/university options (community college recommended for entry)
3. Apply to at least 3 colleges (aim for acceptance by Month 2–3)

**MONTH 2–3 (Acceptance + Application Launch):**
4. Upon first college acceptance, immediately:
   - Complete FAFSA (fafsa.gov) — this triggers Pell Grant eligibility
   - Apply to Prison Education Foundation Scholarship
   - Apply to Save One Life Foundation (if age ≤25)
   - Apply to Transcending Through Education Foundation
   - Apply to state grant (Oregon Opportunity Grant for OR residents)
5. Apply to 5–8 additional scholarships simultaneously (maximize chances)
6. Enroll in college for Fall or Winter semester

**MONTH 4–6 (Award Notifications):**
7. Track all applications; follow up if no notification after 4 weeks
8. Accept all scholarships offered (can layer multiple awards)
9. Complete any required post-award steps (e.g., tuition contribution plans for PEF)

**Realistic Funding Scenario (1st Year):**
- Pell Grant: $6,895
- Oregon Opportunity Grant: $3,500
- Prison Education Foundation: $1,000
- Save One Life (if age ≤25): $5,000–$10,000
- **Total Potential:** $16,395–$21,395 (covers full year at community college + books + living expenses)
