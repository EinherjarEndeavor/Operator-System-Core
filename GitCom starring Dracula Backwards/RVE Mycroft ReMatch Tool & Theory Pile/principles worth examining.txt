Research Findings: Cross-Domain Principles for Your Hook
Domain 1: Trauma-Informed Design Principles
Core Philosophy: Create safety, predictability, and empowerment for users who've experienced systemic trauma

Atomic Principles:

Safety & Predictability: Eliminate aggressive language, maintain consistent layouts, use calming colors and gentle typography
​

Trust & Transparency: Use plain language, make policies accessible, provide honest feedback about what happens next
​

Choice & Control: Give users autonomy over their interaction, easy exits, opt-in features
​

Empowerment & Agency: Frame content to build confidence, avoid blame-based messaging
​

Strength-Based Framing: Describe people by their assets and potential, not deficits; use active voice to name systemic barriers rather than personal failures

Domain 2: Cognitive Load Theory for Web Design
Core Philosophy: Reduce mental effort required to process information

Atomic Principles:

Visual Hierarchy: Use headings, spacing, and size to guide scanning without reading everything
​

Chunking: Break content into small, manageable parts (cards, bullets, sections)
​

Progressive Disclosure: Show only essential information at each step, reveal more when needed
​

One Primary Action Per Page: Too many CTAs cause analysis paralysis

Generous White Space: Creates visual breathing room, reduces overwhelm
​

Familiar Patterns: Use recognized layouts and interactions to reduce learning curve

Immediate Feedback: Confirm actions instantly to keep users oriented
​

Domain 3: AIDA Marketing Formula
Core Philosophy: Guide prospects through Attention → Interest → Desire → Action

Atomic Principles:

Attention: Bold headlines addressing specific pain points, thought-provoking questions, counter-intuitive statements
​

Interest: Explain the problem clearly in terms audience recognizes, share relevant insights demonstrating expertise
​

Desire: Customer success stories with real results, specific outcomes aligned with goals, proof points building credibility
​

Action: Clear specific CTAs, reduced friction, time-sensitive offers creating urgency without pressure, confidence builders like guarantees
​

Don't Skip Stages: Must build interest and desire before asking for action
​

Domain 4: Cialdini's Principles of Persuasion
Core Philosophy: Six psychological triggers that drive "yes"

Atomic Principles:

Reciprocity: Give something valuable first (free info, tools), people feel obligated to give back

Social Proof: Show others like them have benefited; testimonials, numbers, success stories

Authority: Demonstrate expertise through specific knowledge, credentials, or track record
​

Liking: Be human, relatable, use their language, show you understand their world

Commitment & Consistency: Start with small yes, build investment gradually

Scarcity: Create urgency through limited availability or time (use carefully, can trigger distrust)
​

Unity: Emphasize shared identity, "people like us"
​

Domain 5: Strength-Based Messaging for Vulnerable Populations
Core Philosophy: Emphasize strengths, opportunities, power—not deficits

Atomic Principles:

Asset-Based Language: "Constraint-based advantages" not "barriers"
​

People-First Language: "Justice-involved individuals" not "ex-cons"
​

Self-Determination: Frame content around choice, agency, possibility
​

Active Voice: Name systemic barriers explicitly, don't blame individuals
​

Avoid Pity Narratives: Don't define people by their challenges; center their horizons and potential
​

Domain 6: Conversion-Centered Design
Core Philosophy: Guide visitors to one clear goal through strategic design

Atomic Principles:

Create Focus: Remove everything not guiding toward the goal, hide nonessential info
​

Build Structure: Information hierarchy that naturally flows visitors to action
​

Stay Consistent: Unified visual language, cohesive brand across all touchpoints
​

Show Benefits: Visuals and copy that showcase outcomes, not features
​

Draw Attention: Contrasting colors for CTAs, directional cues, negative space around buttons
​

Design for Trust: Testimonials, trust badges, security indicators at decision points

Reduce Friction: Remove steps, simplify checkout, offer guarantees
​

Domain 7: Value Proposition Formulas
Core Philosophy: Clearly communicate relevance, value, and uniqueness

Atomic Principles:

Who-Specific Formula: "The only/best/easy [X] for [specific audience]"
​

Value-Specific Formula: "My product is the one that [specific outcome]"
​

Problem-Solution Format: Name the pain point, then your unique solution
​

Three Components: Define offer clearly + specify who it's for + differentiate your value
​

Domain 8: Microcopy & CTA Best Practices
Core Philosophy: Small words guide big actions

Atomic Principles:

Action Verbs: "Get Your Report" not "Submit"

Task-Specific Language: Describe exactly what happens when clicked
​

Benefit-Driven Copy: Show the outcome, not the process
​

Short & Commanding: 2-5 words max for buttons
​

Reduce Anxiety: Add microcopy around buttons to eliminate concerns
​

Social Proof in Proximity: "Join 5,000+ people" near CTA
​

Domain 9: Form Design Best Practices
Core Philosophy: Make data collection feel effortless and safe

Atomic Principles:

Single Column Layout: Reduces cognitive load, feels less overwhelming

Easiest to Hardest: Start with name/email before sensitive data

Logical Grouping: Related fields together with visual separation

Progressive Disclosure: Multi-step forms, conditional fields appearing based on answers
​

Clear Labels: Explain what you'll do with the data
​

Mark Optional Fields: Reduces perceived burden
​

Domain 10: Legal Framework for Accepting Money
Research Findings on "GAS$" (Gift for Awesome Service):

Pay-What-You-Want Model is legally distinct from both donations and tips:

Tips: Customary payments for services in specific industries (food service, hospitality); subject to special tax treatment but must be reported

Donations: Typically for registered charities; may be tax-deductible for donor
​

Pay-What-You-Want: Voluntary pricing where buyer determines amount, legally valid for digital products and services

Legal Safety for Your Model:

✅ PWYW is legally recognized as a legitimate pricing strategy

✅ After-service payment performs better for high-value offerings
​

✅ Transparency about use of funds increases amounts paid
​

✅ Framing as "support" or "contribution" rather than "donation" avoids charity regulations
​

⚠️ All payments are taxable income regardless of terminology

⚠️ Cannot claim tax-deductibility for payer unless you're a registered 501(c)(3)
​

Recommended Framing: "Voluntary Payment for Services Rendered" or "Pay-What-Feels-Right Contribution"

You can do batch image generation with Gemini either directly via the Gemini CLI’s built‑in image.generate command, or by wiring the Nanobanana extension into the CLI and letting the extension handle image calls. Below is a practical, command‑level walkthrough of both.

1. Batch image generation with Gemini CLI
Gemini CLI exposes image generation via commands like image.generate (names can differ slightly depending on CLI version, but the pattern is similar). Under the hood it calls Nano Banana / flash‑image models such as gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview or gemini-3-pro-image-preview (Nano Banana Pro).

One‑off image generation
The simplest pattern is: prompt → model → write the base64 image to file.

Typical CLI syntax (adapt for your installed CLI; model name is key):

bash
gemini call \
  --model gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview \
  --input "A cyberpunk alley at night, neon reflections on wet pavement, cinematic lighting" \
  --image-out ./out/cyberpunk_alley.png
Key ideas:

--model: use an image‑capable model such as gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview, gemini-3-pro-image-preview (Nano Banana Pro), or gemini-2.5-flash-image-preview depending on what your CLI supports.

--input: plain text prompt, or a JSON file with multimodal content (text + reference images).

--image-out (name may differ, e.g. --output-image or similar): tells the CLI to decode inline image data and write to disk.

If your CLI uses subcommands instead of call, the equivalent is:

bash
gemini image.generate \
  --model gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview \
  --prompt "A logo: stylized silver letter S with electric blue glow" \
  --output ./out/logo_silver_s.png
The exact flag names will follow the pattern used in the CLI, but all it’s doing is wrapping the REST/SDK call illustrated in the docs.
​

Batch generation in a single CLI call (up to ~10 images)
Image models can return multiple images in one response if you structure the contents as multiple “requests” in the same call.
​
​

Most Gemini CLIs allow either:

Repeated --input or --prompt flags, or

An input JSON file containing an array of prompts.

Example using a JSON file:

batch_prompts.json:

json
[
  { "id": "scene-1", "prompt": "Dark fantasy forest with bioluminescent plants, 16:9" },
  { "id": "scene-2", "prompt": "Abandoned spaceship corridor with red emergency lights, 16:9" },
  { "id": "scene-3", "prompt": "Bright minimalist room with a single bonsai on a table, 1:1" }
]
CLI call:

bash
gemini image.generate \
  --model gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview \
  --batch-input ./batch_prompts.json \
  --output-dir ./out/batch_scenes
What usually happens:

The CLI loops over the JSON list and issues one request per prompt.

It writes each result to ./out/batch_scenes/<id>.png (or a similar convention).

You get simple batch behavior without needing the full Batch API.

If your CLI doesn’t have --batch-input, you can still batch by scripting:

bash
while IFS= read -r prompt; do
  slug=$(echo "$prompt" | tr ' ' '_' | tr -cd '[:alnum:]_')
  gemini image.generate \
    --model gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview \
    --prompt "$prompt" \
    --output "./out/$slug.png"
done < prompts.txt
High‑volume batch with Gemini Batch API
When you need hundreds or thousands of images, Google recommends the Batch API, which the CLI can call indirectly (or you invoke via a small script).
​

Core Batch API flow:
​

Prepare a JSONL file where each line is a distinct request:

text
{"key": "logo-1", "request": {"contents":[{"parts":[{"text":"A big letter A surrounded by animals starting with A"}]}], "generation_config":{"responseModalities":["TEXT","IMAGE"]}}}
{"key": "logo-2", "request": {"contents":[{"parts":[{"text":"A big letter B surrounded by animals starting with B"}]}], "generation_config":{"responseModalities":["TEXT","IMAGE"]}}}
Upload JSONL and create a batch job.

The Python sample:
​

python
from google import genai
from google.genai import types

client = genai.Client()

uploaded_file = client.files.upload(
    file="my-batch-image-requests.jsonl",
    config=types.UploadFileConfig(
        display_name="my-batch-image-requests",
        mime_type="jsonl",
    ),
)

file_batch_job = client.batches.create(
    model="gemini-3-pro-image-preview",
    src=uploaded_file.name,
    config={"display_name": "file-image-upload-job-1"},
)
Poll job status until it’s JOB_STATE_SUCCEEDED, then download the result JSONL.
​

Decode each inlineData image and save to files.

You can wrap this in a small script and then create a CLI alias, e.g. gimg-batch my-batch-image-requests.jsonl.

2. Using Gemini CLI with the Nanobanana extension
Nanobanana is an extension that plugs image generation/editing (Nano Banana models) directly into Gemini CLI, giving you extra commands and cleaner ergonomics for images.

Install and connect Nanobanana
Docs for connecting an image extension from a CLI‑oriented tutorial:
​

Typical steps:

Install the extension (global):

bash
gemini extensions install nanobanana
or, if it’s distributed as an npm package or a plugin:

bash
npm install -g @gemini-cli-extensions/nanobanana
The GitHub repo gemini-cli-extensions/nanobanana shows the exact install command.
​

Enable it in the CLI config or project:

bash
gemini extensions enable nanobanana
Some CLIs also support per‑project config, e.g. in .gemini-cli.json:

json
{
  "extensions": {
    "nanobanana": {
      "enabled": true,
      "defaultModel": "gemini-3-pro-image-preview"
    }
  }
}
The “Mastering Gemini CLI – Connect NanoBanana” article describes similar one‑command setup.

Ensure your GEMINI_API_KEY is set and that you have access to the Nano Banana / image models.

Where prompts.jsonl contains one prompt per line:

text

Typical behavior:

The extension fans out requests respecting rate limits (optionally using the Batch API for high volume).

Builds filenames from id or an index, e.g. boxing-1.png.

Offers --concurrency or --max-requests-per-second to avoid throttling.

from google import genai
import os, json

client = genai.Client(api_key=os.environ["GEMINI_API_KEY"])

with open("prompts.json", "r", encoding="utf-8") as f:
    prompts = json.load(f)  # ["prompt 1", "prompt 2", ...]

3. Architecture for “sloppy history → coherent longform + lists/tables”
Here’s a practical pipeline you can build around Gemini:

Step 1: Standardize where raw history lands
Pick a single ingest root, e.g.:

~/knowledge/raw/ with subfolders: gemini_chats/, obsidian_exports/, papers/, saved_web/.

Export Gemini chats (web + CLI) as HTML or JSON; save Obsidian daily notes; save PDFs/web pages there.

The payoff is that Gemini (via API/CLI) and your scripts have one place to look.

Step 2: Daily “digestion” job
Create a daily job (Python or Node) that:

Walks ~/knowledge/raw/ for new/changed files.

For each file:

Run a conversion tool (Tika, pdfplumber, etc.) to get clean text + simple structure.

Send chunks to Gemini with a prompt like:

“You are a document compiler. Rewrite this sloppy conversation into a coherent longform doc with:

A short abstract

Clear section headings

A bullet summary

At the end: a consolidated section ‘Tools, Frameworks, Books’ containing only normalized lists and tables extracted from the text.”

Save outputs as Markdown into ~/knowledge/clean/<date>_<slug>.md.

Gemini is very good at multi‑page synthesis and table creation—this is essentially a lighter‑weight, local Deep Research run over your personal corpus instead of the public web.

Step 3: Build an index over cleaned docs
Use one of your vector DBs (Chroma, Qdrant, Weaviate, etc.) to index the cleaned markdowns, not the raw history.
​

Attach metadata: date, topic, source (Gemini chat vs paper vs note), tags.

Then when you run a new “Deep Research–like” query, your orchestrator can:

Ask Gemini Deep Research Agent about the public web, using google_search and url_context.

In parallel, query your local index for prior docs and feed the relevant chunks into the same request (via file search tools or as context).

Have a final synthesis step that merges “public web findings + personal corpus findings” into one report.
