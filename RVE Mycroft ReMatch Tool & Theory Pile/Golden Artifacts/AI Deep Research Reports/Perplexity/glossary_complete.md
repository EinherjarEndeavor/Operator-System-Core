# THE DANCE OF DEATH: COMPLETE GLOSSARY
## All Jargon, Acronyms, Anatomical Terms, and Biological Concepts Defined

---

## TRAINING & EXERCISE TERMINOLOGY

### Basic Lifting Concepts

**1RM (One-Rep Max)**
- The maximum weight you can lift for exactly one repetition with proper form
- Used to calculate training loads (e.g., 85% 1RM = 85% of your maximum single lift)
- Example: If your bicep curl 1RM is 50 lbs, then 85% = 42.5 lbs

**Concentric Contraction**
- The lifting/shortening phase of a muscle when it contracts against resistance
- Example: Curling a dumbbell UP from waist to shoulder is concentric
- Muscle is contracting and shortening simultaneously

**Eccentric Contraction (also called "negative")**
- The lowering/lengthening phase when a muscle resists the weight being pulled down by gravity
- Example: Lowering the dumbbell FROM shoulder back to waist is eccentric
- Muscle is actively lengthening under tension (resisting)
- Eccentric strength is naturally ~140% of concentric strength (you can lower more than you can lift)

**Isometric Contraction**
- Muscle contraction with NO movement (no shortening or lengthening)
- Example: Holding a plank, holding a weight at the top of a curl without moving
- Creates mechanical tension without any ROM change

**Rep (Repetition)**
- One complete cycle of concentric + eccentric (one lift cycle)
- Example: One bicep curl = one rep (curl up + lower down = 1 rep)
- 10 reps = 10 complete lift cycles

**Set**
- A group of consecutive reps without stopping
- Example: "3 sets of 5 reps" = do 5 reps, rest, do 5 reps, rest, do 5 reps (3 times total)

**RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion)**
- Subjective 1-10 scale of training intensity
- 1-3/10 = very easy, could do many more reps
- 5/10 = moderate, somewhat challenging
- 8/10 = hard, could only do 1-2 more reps
- 10/10 = absolute maximum effort, cannot do another rep

**ROM (Range of Motion)**
- The distance a joint moves through during an exercise
- Full ROM = complete movement from fully extended to fully contracted
- Partial ROM = limited range (e.g., top 1/4 of a bicep curl)
- Longer ROM generally produces more stimulus

**Tempo**
- The speed at which you perform each phase of a lift
- Notation: (Concentric seconds) - (Pause seconds) - (Eccentric seconds)
- Example: 1-0-3 tempo = 1 second up, 0 second pause at top, 3 seconds down
- DoD uses explosive concentrics (1-2 sec) and inertia-resist eccentrics (2-4 sec)

**Time Under Tension (TUT)**
- Total time a muscle spends under load during a set
- Measured in seconds per set
- Example: 10 reps at 1-second concentric + 3-second eccentric = 40 seconds TUT
- Optimal TUT for hypertrophy: 40-70 seconds per set

### Drop Set Terminology

**Drop Set (also called "mechanical drop" or "weight reduction")**
- Immediately reducing weight after reaching muscular failure, then continuing for more reps
- No rest between drops, continuous set
- Example: Curl 45 lbs to failure (10 reps), immediately switch to 35 lbs, continue to failure (12 reps)
- Efficiently extends set and accumulates metabolic stress

**Mechanical Advantage Drop**
- Instead of reducing weight, shift to an easier ROM variation
- Example: Full ROM pull-ups → assisted pull-ups (machine assistance) → negative-only pull-ups
- Useful when dumbbells aren't immediately available

**Muscular Failure (Concentric Failure)**
- Point where muscle cannot produce another complete rep with proper form
- Cannot initiate or complete the lifting (concentric) phase
- Safe to train to; indicates maximum stimulus for that set

**Eccentric Failure**
- Point where muscle cannot eccentrically lower weight with control (e.g., can't control descent on negative)
- Occurs after concentric failure
- Still usable for training but requires spotter for safety

---

### Explosive & Momentum Training

**Explosive Concentric**
- Lifting phase performed with maximum speed and force, generating momentum
- Goal: As FAST as possible while maintaining control
- Creates maximal Type II fiber recruitment
- Generates momentum that will be resisted on eccentric

**Inertia Resist Eccentric**
- Lowering phase where you fight the momentum created by explosive concentric
- Slower than concentric (2-4 seconds), intentionally resisting the movement
- Eccentric load is effectively 110-130% due to momentum resistance
- DoD signature mechanic

**Momentum**
- Physical force generated by moving weight explosively
- In DoD: Explosive concentric creates momentum that inertia-resist eccentric must fight against
- Increases eccentric load without increasing actual weight lifted

**Inertia**
- Object's resistance to change in motion (Newton's 1st law)
- Weight moving downward due to gravity and momentum continues downward; muscle resists this
- "Inertia resist" = fighting the weight's downward momentum, not just gravity

---

### Isolation & Compound Exercises

**Compound Exercise**
- Exercise involving multiple joints and muscle groups
- Example: Squat (ankle, knee, hip joints; quads, hamstrings, glutes, core)
- Typically allows heavier loads

**Isolation Exercise**
- Exercise involving primarily one joint and one primary muscle group
- Example: Bicep curl (elbow joint; biceps primarily)
- Typically lighter loads, more targeted stimulus

**Primary Mover (Prime Mover, Agonist)**
- Muscle that produces the main movement
- Example: In a bicep curl, biceps brachii is primary mover

**Antagonist**
- Muscle opposite the primary mover
- Example: In a bicep curl, triceps are antagonist (relaxes while biceps contract)

**Stabilizer**
- Secondary muscle that supports the primary mover and maintains form
- Example: In a bicep curl, shoulders and forearms stabilize the movement

## ANATOMICAL & PHYSIOLOGICAL TERMS

### Muscle Structure

**Muscle Fiber**
- Individual muscle cell
- Myofibrils within contain contractile proteins (actin and myosin)
- Can be Type I (slow-twitch) or Type II (fast-twitch)

**Sarcomere**
- Functional unit of muscle contraction
- Bounded by Z-discs on either end
- Contains myosin (thick filament) and actin (thin filament)
- Contracts when myosin heads pull actin toward center

**Z-Disc (Z-Line)**
- Boundary structure of sarcomere
- Myosin filaments are anchored here
- Microtrauma to Z-discs signals muscle growth (eccentric training particularly damages these)

**Myofibril**
- Bundle of sarcomeres stacked end-to-end
- Makes up the contractile machinery of muscle
- Multiple myofibrils = one muscle fiber

**Motor Unit**
- Single motor neuron + all muscle fibers it innervates
- Activation of motor unit = contraction of all fibers in that unit
- Larger motor units recruited for heavier loads

**Motor Neuron**
- Nerve cell that carries signal from brain/spinal cord to muscle fibers
- When activated, triggers contraction of all fibers in its motor unit

**Type I Fibers (Slow-Twitch, ST)**
- Muscle fiber optimized for endurance
- Characteristics: Low force production, fatigue-resistant, aerobic metabolism
- Recruitment: Activated first (low loads), recruited throughout movement
- % of body: ~40-50% of leg muscles (variable by muscle)
- Growth response: Smaller hypertrophy gains vs Type II

**Type II Fibers (Fast-Twitch, FT)**
- Muscle fiber optimized for power and strength
- Characteristics: High force production, fatigue quickly, anaerobic metabolism
- Subtypes: IIa (oxidative, moderate fatigue) and IIx (glycolytic, fast fatigue)
- Recruitment: Recruited at high loads or high velocity
- % of body: ~50-60% of leg muscles (variable)
- Growth response: Larger hypertrophy gains with heavy/explosive training

**Henneman Size Principle (Orderly Recruitment)**
- Motor units are recruited in order of size from smallest to largest
- Type I (smaller motor units) always recruited first
- Type II (larger motor units) recruited only when demand increases
- Implication: Must use heavy loads or explosive speed to fully recruit Type II fibers

### Muscle Damage & Repair

**Microtrauma (Muscle Damage)**
- Microscopic tears in muscle fiber structure and connective tissue
- Occurs during eccentric contractions, particularly with novel stimulus
- Triggers repair cascade and growth response
- Different from injury (macrotrauma), which is visible damage

**DOMS (Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness)**
- Muscle soreness that appears 12-48 hours post-exercise, peaks 24-48 hours, resolves 5-7 days
- Caused by microtrauma and inflammatory response
- NOT an indicator of muscle growth (soreness ≠ gains), but often accompanies growth stimulus
- Reduced by Repeated Bout Effect (training same movement repeatedly)

**Inflammatory Response (Inflammation, Inflammatory Cascade)**
- Immune system response to microtrauma
- Process: Damage → cytokine release → immune cell infiltration → cleanup and repair
- Key inflammation markers: TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β (cytokines)
- Necessary for growth signal; excessive inflammation impairs recovery

**Satellite Cells (Muscle Satellite Cells, Myogenic Progenitor Cells)**
- Muscle stem cells adjacent to muscle fibers
- Activated by training stimulus and repair signals
- Fuse with existing muscle fibers, adding nuclei
- New nuclei increase capacity for protein synthesis (more ribosomes available)
- Critical for long-term hypertrophy; without them, muscle growth plateaus

**Nucleus (Myonucleus)**
- Genetic control center of muscle fiber containing DNA
- One myonucleus oversees ~100 micrometers of muscle fiber (myonuclear domain)
- More nuclei = greater capacity for protein synthesis per fiber
- Satellite cell fusion adds nuclei permanently (retained even with detraining)

**Protein Breakdown (Proteolysis, Catabolism)**
- Process of breaking down muscle proteins into amino acids
- Always occurring; exceeded by protein synthesis during growth
- Elevated immediately post-exercise (if no protein consumed)
- Reduced by amino acid availability (especially leucine and total protein)

## BIOCHEMICAL & MOLECULAR TERMS

### Growth Signaling Pathways

**mTORC1 (Mechanistic Target of Rapamycin Complex 1)**
- Master molecular "switch" for muscle growth
- Activated by: Heavy loads, mechanical tension, amino acids (especially leucine)
- When active: Signals ribosomes to synthesize more protein
- When inactive: Protein synthesis suppressed
- Key downstream effect: p70S6K activation

**mTOR (Mechanistic Target of Rapamycin)**
- Parent protein to mTORC1 and mTORC2
- Central regulator of cell growth, metabolism, anabolism

**p70S6K (Ribosomal S6 Kinase)**
- Enzyme activated by mTORC1
- Phosphorylates ribosomal protein S6
- Effect: Increases ribosomal capacity for protein synthesis

**PI3K/AKT Signaling Pathway**
- Secondary growth pathway activated by mechanical stimulus and growth factors
- AKT phosphorylates and inhibits GSK-3β (which normally suppresses protein synthesis)
- Result: Increased protein synthesis
- Often works parallel to mTOR pathway

**AMPK (AMP-Activated Protein Kinase)**
- Energy sensor ("metabolic master switch")
- Activated by: Low energy (high AMP:ATP ratio), endurance exercise, caloric deficit
- When active: Suppresses mTORC1, reduces anabolism, increases catabolism
- High-intensity HIIT activates AMPK more than steady-state training

**Mechanotransduction**
- Process of converting mechanical force into chemical signals
- Heavy load → mechanical stress on cell membrane → sensor proteins activated → molecular cascade initiated
- Cellular "language" that translates physical stress into growth signal

**Mechanoreceptors (Mechanosensors)**
- Proteins in muscle cell membranes that sense mechanical force
- Types: Integrins, calcium channels, stretch-activated ion channels, Piezo channels
- Function: Detect tension/stretch and initiate signal cascade

### Protein Synthesis & Amino Acids

**MPS (Muscle Protein Synthesis)**
- Process of ribosomes building new muscle proteins (primarily myosin and actin)
- Spiked by: Mechanical stimulus + amino acid availability (especially leucine)
- Timeline: Begins 30 min post-exercise, peaks ~90 min, elevated for ~3 hours with protein intake
- **Net muscle growth = MPS > MPB (protein breakdown)**

**mRNA (Messenger RNA)**
- Molecule carrying genetic instructions from DNA
- Transcribed from DNA in nucleus
- Transported to cytoplasm where ribosomes read it
- Ribosomes synthesize protein according to mRNA instructions

**Ribosome**
- Cellular machinery that reads mRNA and assembles proteins
- Contains rRNA (ribosomal RNA) and ribosomal proteins
- Multiple ribosomes on one mRNA = polyribosome
- Higher ribosome count (from satellite cell fusion) = greater MPS capacity

**Myosin (Myosin Heavy Chain, MHC)**
- Thick filament protein; primary structural and contractile protein in muscle
- Major component of sarcomere
- Comprises ~50% of muscle protein
- Hypertrophy = increased myosin content per fiber

**Actin**
- Thin filament protein; primary structural and contractile protein in muscle
- Interacts with myosin heads to create contraction
- Comprises ~20% of muscle protein

**Leucine (L-Leucine)**
- Branch-chain amino acid (BCAA); most anabolic amino acid
- Strongest activator of mTORC1 independent of other amino acids
- Target: 2-3 grams per meal to maximally trigger MPS
- Rich sources: Whey protein, eggs, meat, dairy

**Essential Amino Acids (EAA)**
- 9 amino acids that cannot be synthesized by body; must be consumed
- Required for MPS to occur
- Complete proteins contain all 9 EAAs
- Examples: Leucine, isoleucine, valine (BCAAs), lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, histidine

**Branch-Chain Amino Acids (BCAA)**
- 3 essential amino acids: Leucine, Isoleucine, Valine
- Leucine most important for mTORC1 activation
- Found abundantly in dairy, eggs, meat
- Isolated BCAA supplementation generally unnecessary if total protein adequate

**MPB (Muscle Protein Breakdown)**
- Rate of muscle protein degradation
- Always occurring; elevated immediately post-exercise if no amino acids available
- Suppressed by: Protein intake (amino acids), low cortisol, adequate energy
- Net muscle gain = MPS > MPB

### Energy & Metabolism

**ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)**
- Primary energy currency of cells
- When one phosphate is removed = ADP + energy released
- Regenerated by: Aerobic metabolism (efficient), anaerobic metabolism (fast), phosphocreatine system
- Muscle contraction = ATP → ADP + P + Energy

**Lactate (Lactic Acid)**
- Byproduct of anaerobic metabolism during high-intensity exercise
- Accumulates in muscle cells, lowers pH, creates "burn" sensation
- NOT the cause of DOMS (debunked); causes temporary fatigue (clears within hours)
- Contributes to metabolic stress signal that triggers growth

**H+ Ions (Hydrogen Ions, Proton Accumulation)**
- Released when lactate is produced and ATP is broken down
- Lowers muscle pH (acidosis)
- Contributes to fatigue sensation and metabolic stress signal
- Accumulation triggers growth factor release

**Metabolites (Training Metabolites)**
- Byproducts of intense muscle activity
- Include: Lactate, H+ ions, phosphate (Pi), other signaling molecules
- Accumulation in muscle cell signals growth via osmotic pressure and chemical signaling
- Metabolic stress mechanism of hypertrophy

**Aerobic Metabolism (Oxidative Metabolism)**
- Energy production using oxygen
- Efficient (produces ~36-38 ATP per glucose)
- Used during low-intensity, longer-duration exercise
- Primarily feeds Type I fibers

**Anaerobic Metabolism (Glycolytic Metabolism)**
- Energy production without oxygen
- Fast but inefficient (~2 ATP per glucose)
- Used during high-intensity, short-duration exercise
- Primarily feeds Type II fibers
- Produces lactate and H+ ions

**Creatine Phosphate System (Phosphocreatine, PCr)**
- Rapid ATP regeneration system for first ~10 seconds of intense exercise
- Creatine phosphate donates phosphate to ADP → ATP
- Regenerated during rest between sets
- Supplemental creatine increases PCr storage, enhancing ATP availability

**Glycogen**
- Stored carbohydrate in muscles and liver
- Broken down to glucose for energy during exercise
- Depleted with high training volume; replenished with carbohydrate intake
- Necessary for optimal performance in high-frequency training

**Cortisol**
- Stress hormone produced by adrenal glands
- Elevated by: Physical training, psychological stress, poor sleep, low energy
- Effects: Catabolic (promotes protein breakdown), suppresses MPS, elevates blood glucose
- Chronically elevated = impaired recovery, reduced growth, relapse risk (critical for addiction recovery)
- Managed by: Adequate sleep, deload weeks, caloric adequacy, stress management

**Testosterone**
- Anabolic hormone supporting MPS and muscle growth
- Naturally elevated by: Heavy training, adequate calories, sleep, low stress
- Enhanced by: Adequate zinc, vitamin D, healthy fats
- Suppressed by: Overtraining, caloric deficit, poor sleep, elevated cortisol

**IGF-1 (Insulin-Like Growth Factor 1)**
- Anabolic hormone produced by liver and muscle
- Stimulates MPS, satellite cell activation, growth
- Elevated by: Training stimulus, adequate calories, sufficient sleep
- Local muscle IGF-1 particularly important for hypertrophy

**Insulin**
- Hormone regulating blood glucose and nutrient partitioning
- Elevated by: Carbohydrate/protein intake
- Anabolic: Suppresses breakdown, promotes glucose and amino acid uptake
- Necessary for optimal MPS response (indirect effect via nutrient availability)

## RECOVERY & ADAPTATION

**Recovery**
- Process of restoring muscle to pre-exercise state and building larger
- Includes: Immediate recovery (minutes), short-term recovery (hours-48 hours), and long-term adaptation (days-weeks)
- Complete recovery requires protein synthesis completion + energy replenishment + hormonal normalization

**Overtraining (Overtraining Syndrome)**
- Chronic state of accumulated fatigue from excessive volume/frequency without adequate recovery
- Symptoms: Stalled strength, persistent soreness, mood decline, sleep disruption, elevated resting HR, suppressed appetite, impaired immunity
- Causes: High frequency without deload, insufficient protein/calories, poor sleep, high stress
- Treatment: Deload week (50% volume, 60% intensity), increased sleep, increased calories, stress reduction

**Overreaching (Planned Overtraining)**
- Intentional short-term increase in training stress beyond normal capacity
- Designed to trigger adaptation during recovery
- Must be followed by deload to consolidate gains
- Acceptable for weeks 1-3 of each cycle; unsustainable long-term

**Repeated Bout Effect (RBE)**
- Adaptation mechanism where second exposure to same stimulus produces less damage and faster recovery
- Timeline: 50% damage reduction by 2nd session, 70% by 3rd session, 80%+ after sustained training
- Lasts weeks; protects muscle when training same group frequently
- Mechanism: Neural efficiency, mechanical adaptation, cellular protection (antioxidants, heat shock proteins)

**Deload (Deload Week)**
- Planned week of reduced training volume/intensity to allow CNS and systemic recovery
- Typical structure: 50% volume, 60% intensity, same movement patterns
- Frequency: Every 3-4 weeks (week 4 of each cycle)
- Benefits: Cortisol reset, satellite cell integration, hormonal recovery, prevents overtraining
- Essential for sustainability of high-frequency training

**Adaptation**
- Process of body becoming more efficient at stress stimulus
- Short-term: Neural adaptation (improved motor recruitment, faster)
- Long-term: Structural adaptation (muscle growth, stronger)
- Requires: Progressive overload (gradually increasing stimulus) to continue

**Progressive Overload**
- Gradual increase in training stimulus over time
- Methods: Increase weight, increase reps, increase sets, decrease rest periods, improve tempo
- Without progressive overload: Adaptation plateaus, gains stall
- Example: Week 1 max effort 3 reps at 45 lbs → Week 3 max effort 5 reps at 45 lbs → Week 5 max effort 3 reps at 50 lbs

## TRAINING SPLIT & PERIODIZATION

**Split (Training Split)**
- Division of muscle groups across weekly sessions
- Examples: Upper/lower split, push/pull/legs, full-body, body part split

**Upper/Lower Split**
- Training week divided into upper body days and lower body days
- Typical: Upper/Lower/Upper/Lower/Rest (or with more frequency)
- Advantage: Allows 2x/week per muscle, higher frequency sustainable

**Push/Pull/Legs (PPL)**
- Divides muscles by movement pattern: pushing, pulling, leg movements
- Example: Push (chest/shoulders/triceps), Pull (back/biceps), Legs (quads/hams/glutes)
- Typically 6 days: Push/Pull/Legs repeated 2x/week

**Full-Body Split**
- Train all major muscle groups in each session
- Typically 3x/week (every other day)
- Advantage: More frequent per muscle, less time per session
- Disadvantage: Lower volume per muscle per session

**Bro Split (Body Part Split)**
- Each session focuses on single muscle group (e.g., Monday = chest, Tuesday = back, etc.)
- Typically 5-6 days/week
- Frequency: 1x/week per muscle (low frequency, requires higher volume per session)
- Disadvantage: Low frequency limits MPS stimulus accumulation

**Mesocycle**
- Medium-term training block, typically 3-4 weeks
- Followed by deload week
- Repeating mesocycles = macrocycle

**Macrocycle**
- Long-term training plan, typically 12 weeks or longer
- Multiple mesocycles (e.g., 4 mesocycles of 3 weeks + deload = 16-week macrocycle)

**Microcycle**
- Short-term training cycle, typically 1 week
- Building block of mesocycle

**Block Periodization**
- Training divided into distinct blocks, each emphasizing different quality
- Example: Accumulation block (volume), intensification block (strength), realization block (power)
- Typically 4-6 weeks per block

**Linear Periodization**
- Gradual progression from high-volume/low-intensity to low-volume/high-intensity
- Used for strength/power sports
- Less optimal for pure hypertrophy

**Daily Undulating Periodization (DUP)**
- Varies intensity/volume daily within same mesocycle
- Example: Monday heavy (low reps), Wednesday moderate (medium reps), Friday light (high reps)
- Optimal for balanced hypertrophy + strength development

## TECHNICAL PERFORMANCE METRICS

**Strength**
- Maximum force production
- Measured by: 1RM weight, reps at given load, force dynamometry
- Improved by: Heavy loads (85%+ 1RM), low reps (1-6), rest-pause training

**Power**
- Rate of force production (force over time)
- Measured by: Jump height, bar velocity, time to peak force
- Improved by: Explosive movements, lighter loads moved fast
- Distinct from strength (strong doesn't necessarily mean powerful)

**Hypertrophy**
- Increase in muscle fiber cross-sectional area
- Measured by: Circumference, ultrasound, photogrammetry
- Improved by: Moderate-high loads (65-85% 1RM), higher reps (6-15+), metabolic stress, consistent stimulus

**Muscular Endurance**
- Ability to perform reps against resistance without fatigue
- Measured by: Reps until failure at submaximal load
- Improved by: Higher reps (15+), short rest periods, metabolic conditioning

**Work Capacity**
- Ability to perform large volume of work in given time
- Measured by: Total reps × weight in session
- Improved by: Increased frequency, higher reps, shorter rest periods

**Cardiovascular Endurance (VO2 Max, Aerobic Capacity)**
- Ability to sustain aerobic effort
- Measured by: VO2 max (ml/kg/min), lactate threshold, heart rate recovery
- Improved by: Steady-state cardio (20-30 min), interval training

**Rate of Force Development (RFD)**
- How quickly muscle can produce force
- Measured by: Time to peak force (milliseconds)
- Improved by: Explosive movements, power training
- Important for athletic performance, injury prevention

## NUTRITION TERMINOLOGY

**Caloric Surplus (Bulk, Hypertrophy Phase)**
- Consuming more calories than expended
- Typical surplus: 300-500 kcal/day
- Supports muscle gain while minimizing fat gain
- Typical rate: +0.5-1 lb/week bodyweight

**Caloric Deficit (Cut, Cutting Phase)**
- Consuming fewer calories than expended
- Typical deficit: 300-500 kcal/day
- Supports fat loss while minimizing muscle loss
- Higher protein critical to preserve muscle (2.2+ g/kg)
- Typical rate: -0.5-1 lb/week bodyweight

**Macronutrients (Macros)**
- Three primary nutrient categories: Protein, carbohydrate, fat

**Protein (Dietary Protein)**
- Macronutrient providing amino acids for MPS
- Target for hypertrophy: 1.6-2.2 g/kg bodyweight
- Sources: Animal (whey, meat, eggs, dairy), plant (legumes, nuts, seeds)

**Carbohydrate (Carbs)**
- Macronutrient providing glucose for energy and muscle glycogen
- Target for training days: 5-7 g/kg bodyweight
- Target for off days: 3-4 g/kg bodyweight
- Sources: Grains (rice, oats, pasta), fruits, vegetables, legumes

**Fat (Dietary Fat)**
- Macronutrient providing energy and supporting hormone synthesis
- Target: 0.3-0.5 g/kg bodyweight (minimum)
- Important for: Testosterone synthesis, vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), satiety
- Sources: Oils, nuts, seeds, fatty fish, avocado

**Micronutrients (Vitamins & Minerals)**
- Essential nutrients required in small amounts
- Critical for recovery, immunity, hormonal health
- Examples: Vitamin D, zinc, magnesium, iron, calcium

**Vitamin D**
- Fat-soluble vitamin; acts as hormone
- Functions: Testosterone support, immune function, calcium absorption
- Target: 2000-4000 IU/day (or blood level 30-50 ng/mL)
- Deficiency common, especially in those with limited sun exposure

**Zinc**
- Mineral essential for testosterone synthesis and immune function
- Target: 30-50 mg/day for men
- Sources: Meat, shellfish, nuts, seeds, legumes
- Deficiency impairs recovery and hormone production

**Magnesium**
- Mineral supporting 300+ enzymatic reactions, especially relevant for muscle function, sleep
- Target: 400-500 mg/day
- Functions: Muscle relaxation, nervous system regulation, ATP production, sleep quality
- Sources: Nuts, seeds, dark leafy greens, legumes

**Whey Protein (Whey Isolate, Whey Concentrate)**
- Complete protein from milk; contains all 9 EAAs, high in leucine
- Characteristics: Fast-digesting, 20-25g per scoop, relatively inexpensive
- Best used post-workout for rapid MPS spike
- Cost: ~$0.50-1.50 per serving (depending on brand/quality)

**Casein Protein**
- Milk protein; micellar casein forms curds in stomach, slow release
- Characteristics: Slow-digesting (6-8 hours), complete amino acid profile
- Best used pre-sleep or in snacks
- Useful for overnight MPS maintenance

**Creatine Monohydrate**
- Supplement providing creatine (naturally synthesized in body from amino acids)
- Function: Increases phosphocreatine stores, enhancing ATP regeneration
- Dosing: 5g daily (no loading phase required)
- Effects: +1-2 lbs bodyweight (water + muscle), +5-10% strength, well-researched safety
- Cost: ~$5-10/month

**Beta-Alanine**
- Amino acid; increases muscle carnosine (pH buffer)
- Dosing: 3-5g/day split into 3 doses (5g single dose can cause tingling)
- Effects: Improved reps in 6-15 rep range, ~5-10% more reps
- Requires 3-4 weeks loading for effect
- Cost: ~$10-15/month

**Citrulline Malate**
- Amino acid reducing fatigue, increasing blood flow
- Dosing: 6-8g pre-workout
- Effects: Increased reps, improved pump sensation, reduced DOMS
- Cost: ~$10-20/month

**Tart Cherry Extract**
- Polyphenol from tart cherry fruit
- Function: Reduces inflammation, aids DOMS recovery
- Dosing: 500mg post-workout
- Effects: ~30% DOMS reduction
- Cost: ~$12-15/month

**Omega-3 (EPA/DHA, Fish Oil)**
- Long-chain polyunsaturated fats; powerful anti-inflammatory
- Dosing: 2-3g combined EPA+DHA daily
- Functions: Reduce DOMS, cardiovascular health, neurological support, reduce inflammation
- Sources: Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel), fish oil supplements, algae supplements (vegan)
- Cost: ~$10-20/month

**BCAAs (Branched-Chain Amino Acids Supplement)**
- Isolated leucine, isoleucine, valine in supplement form
- Generally unnecessary if adequate total protein consumed
- Useful only for fasted training (prevents catabolism)
- Cost: $20-40/month (expensive for what they do)

## MEASUREMENT & MONITORING

**Body Composition**
- Proportion of lean mass (muscle, bone, organs) vs fat mass
- Measured by: Scale (imprecise), tape measurements, ultrasound, DEXA, calipers
- More relevant than scale weight alone

**Lean Mass (Lean Body Mass, LBM, Fat-Free Mass)**
- All body mass except fat
- Includes: Muscle, bone, organs, water
- Relevant measure for training progress (scale weight can be deceiving due to water/glycogen fluctuations)

**Body Fat Percentage**
- Proportion of total bodyweight that is fat
- Typical ranges: 10-20% (athletic), 20-25% (average), 25%+ (higher)
- Measured via multiple methods with varying accuracy

**Circumference Measurement**
- Tape measurement of body parts (arm, leg, chest, waist)
- Quick, inexpensive progress marker
- Measures hypertrophy (if arm circumference increases, hypertrophy likely)
- Limitation: Includes water, glycogen, fat changes

**Strength Testing**
- Estimated 1RM: Calculate maximum weight from submaximal lift
- Formula: (Reps × 0.033) + Load = Estimated 1RM
- Example: 10 reps of 100 lbs = (10 × 0.033) + 100 = 100.33 lbs 1RM estimate
- Useful for tracking progress without maxing out repeatedly (safer)

**Resting Heart Rate (RHR)**
- Heart rate measured immediately upon waking
- Elevated RHR (+10+ bpm) indicates: Overtraining, insufficient recovery, illness
- Decreased RHR = improved recovery, cardiovascular fitness
- Track daily as overtraining indicator

**Evidence-Based Training**
- Training principles supported by peer-reviewed research (RCTs, meta-analyses)
- Contrasts with: Anecdotal, gym bro, unproven methods
- Your protocol: Heavily evidence-based; all mechanisms (mechanical tension, metabolic stress, muscle damage) validated in literature

**Biomechanics**
- Study of forces and motion in living systems
- Relevant to training: Leverage, joint angles, load distribution, injury risk
- Example: Full ROM produces more stimulus than partial ROM (longer lever arm, greater stretch)

**Physiology**
- Study of how living systems function
- Muscle physiology: How contraction occurs, energy systems, adaptation
- Recovery physiology: Sleep, hormones, immune function, protein synthesis

**Periodization Strategy**
- Scientific approach to training varying stimulus over time
- Goals: Maximize adaptation, prevent plateaus, prevent overtraining, long-term progression
- Your protocol: 3-week accumulation + 1-week deload (mesocycle repetition)
