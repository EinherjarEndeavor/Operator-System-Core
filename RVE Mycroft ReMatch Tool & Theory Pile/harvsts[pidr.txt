This is an arms race. The key is to not look like a bot. You must act human and distribute your traffic.

1. The Proxy Rotation Strategy (Essential):

Never Use Your Own IP: Your first step is to route all your script's traffic through proxies.
Tool: Use the requests library in Python with a Session object configured to use proxies.
Source of Proxies:
Free: You can find lists of free proxies online, but they are slow, unreliable, and often already blacklisted. Good for testing, bad for production.
Paid (Recommended for Serious Work): Services like Luminati, Smartproxy, or Oxylabs provide massive pools of high-quality residential and datacenter IPs. They offer APIs that automatically rotate IPs for you. This is the single most effective upgrade you can make.
Implementation: Your script should be configured to use a new proxy for every 10-20 requests, or when it hits a rate limit.
2. User-Agent & Header Rotation:

The Problem: A script using the default Python requests User-Agent is instantly flagged.
The Solution: Create a list of 20-30 real User-Agent strings (from https://www.whatismybrowser.com/guides/the-latest-user-agent/). For every request, your script should randomly pick one from the list.
Library: Use the fake-useragent library to automate this.
Full Headers: Don't just rotate the User-Agent. Rotate a full set of realistic headers (Accept, Accept-Language, Accept-Encoding, Connection, Upgrade-Insecure-Requests, etc.).
3. Intelligent Throttling and Human-like Behavior:

Random Delays: The most basic defense. Use time.sleep(random.uniform(2, 8)) between requests. A bot requests at a fixed interval; a human does not.
Session Management: Use requests.Session() to persist cookies and handle logins if you ever decide to use semi-private accounts (not recommended for full anonymity, but sometimes necessary). This makes your requests look like they're coming from a single browser.
Clicks and Scrolls: When using selenium or playwright for browser automation, don't just jump to elements. Simulate mouse movements, random scrolls, and small delays before "clicking." Libraries like pyautogui can help with this.
4. CAPTCHA Solving (The Nuclear Option):

When you get a CAPTCHA, you've already lost. The goal is to avoid them entirely. But if you must...
Tool: Use a third-party CAPTCHA solving service like 2Captcha or Anti-Captcha.
How it Works: Your script encounters a CAPTCHA. It takes a screenshot of the CAPTCHA, sends it to the service's API (usually costing a fraction of a cent per solve), and receives the text solution back. Your script then types the solution into the form and submits.
Implementation: This is complex to integrate but is the only automated way to bypass many CAPTCHAs. It should be your last resort, as it increases the cost and complexity of your operation significantly.
